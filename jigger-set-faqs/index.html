Quick answer

A jigger set is the most essential measuring tool for consistent cocktails. Look for a double-sided jigger with clear, etched markings in standard bar measurements (like 1 oz / 2 oz or ¾ oz / 1½ oz), made from heavy-gauge stainless steel for durability and balance. This FAQ covers what to look for, how to read them, and whether you need a set or just one.

What sizes do jiggers come in?

Most jiggers are double-ended, pairing two different volumes. Common combos include 1 oz on one side and 2 oz on the other, or ¾ oz and 1½ oz for smaller pours. You’ll also find Japanese-style jiggers in 1 oz / 2 oz with an inner line for ½ oz, and specialty sets that include a micro-jigger (¼ oz / ½ oz) and a larger 2 oz / 3 oz. For home use, a 1 oz / 2 oz jigger covers 90% of recipes. If you frequently make classic cocktails like a daiquiri (2 oz spirit, 1 oz lime, ¾ oz syrup), having a side that measures ¾ oz is very handy. Beginners often start with a 1 oz / 2 oz double jigger and later add a smaller one for bitters or syrups.

Stainless steel vs. copper vs. glass: which material is best?

Stainless steel is the industry standard. It’s durable, rust-resistant, and doesn’t react with acidic ingredients. Look for a heavy-gauge option that feels solid in hand, cheaper thin jiggers can dent or get out of shape. OXO’s angle-adjustable jigger and Cocktail Kingdom’s Japanese-style are popular for their sturdy feel. Copper jiggers are beautiful and traditional, but they need more care. Copper can tarnish and react with citrus, affecting taste. They’re best for display or occasional use. Glass jiggers let you see the measurement but are fragile and can slip when wet. Stainless remains the most practical choice for daily bartending.

How do I read measurements on a jigger?

Measurements can be etched, engraved, stamped, or printed. Etched or engraved lines inside the cup are best because they won’t wear off and are easy to read at a glance. Some jiggers use horizontal lines inside the larger cup for fractional pours (e.g., ½ oz inside a 1 oz cup). Others, like the classic double jigger, have a rim that tells you the volume for one side and a distinct line for the other. Look for markings in ounces or a combination of ounces and milliliters. US-based recipes use oz, but some modern jiggers include metric. Avoid painted or printed markings that fade after a few washes. A well-designed jigger will have clear, deep engraving so you can fill accurately without squinting.

Should I buy a single jigger or a full set?

A single double-jigger (1 oz / 2 oz) is enough to start. But a set of two or three jiggers gives you more flexibility. For example, one jigger with ¾ oz / 1½ oz and another with 1 oz / 2 oz covers nearly every recipe without guesswork. Some sets also include a micro-jigger for measuring bar spoons of syrup or bitters. If you entertain often or like making stirred drinks that call for precise fractions, a three-piece set is worth considering. But don’t buy a cheap six-piece set that includes a shaker and strainer you won’t use. A curated set from a reputable barware brand (like Cocktail Kingdom or Barfly) will have better build quality and more practical sizes.

What about weight, balance, and pour spouts?

A jigger should feel balanced when full. Heavier bases prevent tipping. Look for a bottom-heavy design or one with a wide foot. Thin, top-heavy jiggers are prone to spilling. Also, check if the pour spout is smooth and slightly tapered, it helps you pour cleanly without dripping down the side. Japanese-style jiggers are often taller and narrower, which gives a more precise pour but can be less stable. Western-style jiggers are shorter and wider, making them easier to steady. Some jiggers have a convex or concave rim for better finger grip. If you have larger hands, a jigger with a slightly flared top is more comfortable. Test the weight if possible; a jigger that feels good in your hand will be used more often.

How do I care for my jigger set?

Most stainless steel jiggers are dishwasher-safe, but hand washing with warm soapy water keeps the engraving clear and prevents water spots. Dry immediately to avoid mineral deposits. Copper jiggers need special polish and should never go in the dishwasher. Glass jiggers are fragile; hand wash gently. Store jiggers in a drawer with a soft liner or hang them on a bar rail. Avoid stacking them wet, they can stick together or develop rust spots if not fully dry. A simple weekly wipe-down with a soft cloth is all most stainless jiggers need to stay shiny for years.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most common size for a cocktail jigger?

The most common double jigger size is 1 oz on one side and 2 oz on the other. This covers standard cocktail build ratios like 2 oz spirit, 1 oz modifier, 1 oz citrus.

Are Japanese jiggers better than Western ones?

Japanese jiggers are typically taller and narrower, with ultra-precise etched lines inside. They offer greater accuracy for smaller pours but can be less stable on a counter. Western jiggers are shorter, wider, and easier to grip, better for speed and balance. Choose based on your pouring style.

Can I use a shot glass instead of a jigger?

A shot glass often works in a pinch, but most are not calibrated to bar-standard measurements. A shot glass might hold 1.5 oz, not 1 or 2 oz, and lacks fractional markings. A proper jigger gives you consistent ratios, which is why professionals use them.

Why do some jiggers have a flat rim and some a slanted rim?

A flat rim allows you to scrape off excess with a knife for a level measurement, while a slanted rim (bell-shaped) directs liquid down the back side for a cleaner pour. Slanted rims are often preferred for speed, flat rims for precision. Many high-end jiggers now have a hybrid design.

Do I need a jigger with milliliter markings?

If you follow US recipes, ounces are fine. If you use international recipes (especially from Europe or Australia), milliliters are very helpful. Many newer jiggers include both units, look for ones with both scales printed inside.

What should I look for in a budget-friendly jigger set?

Even at a lower price, choose fully stainless steel with etched markings (not printed) and a weighted base. Avoid sets that come with many extra tools you won’t use. A simple two-jigger set (1 oz/2 oz plus ¾ oz/1½ oz) from a known brand like OXO or Barfly will perform well for years.

In shortA good jigger set is a small investment that pays off in every drink you pour. Focus on sturdy stainless steel, clear etched markings, and sizes that match your most-used recipes. Avoid flashy gimmicks or overly cheap sets, you’ll end up replacing them. With the right jigger, you’ll taste the consistency immediately.
